# Playtesting

# 1. Pre-Playtest Design Intent

The **Player goal** is to add honey to their porridge. In order to complete that goal, the player needs to solve the following puzzles:
1. Figure out the first thing they need to acquire. which is honey.
2. Figure out how to evade the bee. Bee's don't like water/rivers.
3. Realize they can no longer get back to their home after losing the bee. Figure out how to get home after they accidentally release the bear.

How I **guide the player**:
1. Short UI text that is also narrated to them over audio.
2. Locking them into a small space.
3. Making the collectible honey actor stand out by being the only item of its color in the game.
4. Making the way you release the bear, knocking over trees, a hint. Because it is very similar to how to get back to your cabin.

**playtest goals**
1. If there is a way to convey that the player wants honey without using text or narrative audio.
2. If it's too short.
3. If the graphics conveyed what the creatures are.
4. If the tyrant speeds are challenging.

# 3. Observation Notes

[raw notes taken during playtest](./PlaytestLog.md) are compiled in the linked separate file.

Summary of the playtests:
- Playtesters always found the first goal, the honey, within the first 20 seconds.
- About 80% of players went directly to the cabin door after collecting the honey. With a "what do I do now" pause after realizing the door was not open.
- A few players noted the invisible wall disappearing after the honey was collecting was confusing. Paul helped me troubleshoot design ideas. Yet, I decided to not go with the idea of "a wall of bees" due to feeling that's even more confusing.
- It was common that they hesitated after releasing the bear. Weiping had the most difficulty figuring out what to do after releasing the bear. She mentioned, that in hindsight, the bear knocking over the tree to make a bridge made sense due to it being exactly the same as how the bear is released. This mimicing was an intended design decision that maybe 40% of playertesters picked up on. This design worked well for that 40%.
- After March17th, the bee tyrant was made faster. The playtesters after that date no longer mentioned the bee's movement felt wrong.
- After implementing feedback from the March 19th playtest. Elijah noted that the door to the cabin was now completely missed as the door was being hidden by the bridge.

# 4. Design Reflection

1. The bee nest, bee and bear art worked. They were always identified.
2. Time of game completion varied wildly; from about 1 minute to over 6 minutes.
3. The tyrant speeds seemed to be correct after the first playtest.
4. Players always were quick to understand the goal of acquiring the honey. They immediately went back to the cabin to see if the door was now opened. They realized it was still closed and they had to find another way.
5. My design that they need to get rid of the bee before opening the door did not come across to any of the playtesters. The idea that bees don't like water didn't cross any of the playtesters minds. They only figured it out through trial and error. I tried to think of a way to teach the player that bees don't water. But couldn't think of any that would be easily do-able.
6. The main confusion was what to do after the bear was released. Some players were able to quickly solve the puzzle after seeing what happened after the players were released. Some got lucky. And some were stumped for a solid 3 or 4 minutes.
7. Even though the bear creating a bridge puzzle caused confusion, I felt this was worth leaving. This is due to the retrospect the players that struggled with it mentioned after finally solving it. Made me feel that the design choice of showing the bear knocking over trees to teach the player that's what the bear does, was effective.
8. I'd figure out a better way to transition from the bee tyrant to the bear tyrant. Players were able to figure out the transition fairly quickly due to the limited playspace. But limited playspace is not the design choice that gives the player the fun I was going for.
